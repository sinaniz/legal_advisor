Employment Law Basics

Understand your workplace rights, protections against discrimination, wage requirements, and what to do if your rights are violated.

Types of Employment Relationships

Understanding your employment classification is crucial as it affects your rights, benefits, and tax obligations.

Employee vs. Independent Contractor

Employees typically:

  • Work under the direction and control of the employer
  • Use employer-provided tools and equipment
  • Receive regular wages or salary
  • Have taxes withheld from their paychecks
  • May receive benefits like health insurance and paid time off
  • Are covered by most employment laws

Independent contractors typically:

  • Control how they perform their work
  • Use their own tools and equipment
  • Are paid by project or contract
  • Pay their own self-employment taxes
  • Don't receive employee benefits
  • Have limited coverage under employment laws

Misclassification Warning

Employers sometimes misclassify employees as independent contractors to avoid taxes and benefits. This is illegal. The Department of Labor and IRS use multiple factors to determine proper classification, not just what your contract states.

At-Will Employment

Most U.S. employees work "at-will," meaning:

  • Either party can end the employment relationship at any time
  • Termination can occur with or without cause
  • No notice is legally required (though often given as a courtesy)

Important exceptions to at-will employment include:

  • Employment contracts specifying duration or termination terms
  • Collective bargaining agreements (union contracts)
  • Terminations that violate anti-discrimination laws
  • Terminations in retaliation for exercising legal rights
  • Terminations that violate public policy

Wage and Hour Laws

The Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) establishes minimum wage, overtime pay, recordkeeping, and youth employment standards.

Minimum Wage

The federal minimum wage is $7.25 per hour, but many states and cities have higher rates. Employers must pay whichever is highest (federal, state, or local).

Overtime Pay

Non-exempt employees must receive overtime pay at 1.5 times their regular rate for hours worked beyond 40 in a workweek. Key points about overtime:

  • Overtime is calculated weekly, not daily (except in some states like California)
  • Employers can't avoid overtime by averaging hours across multiple weeks
  • Unauthorized overtime must still be paid (though employers can discipline for rule violations)

Exempt vs. Non-Exempt Classification

Exempt employees (not entitled to overtime) must meet ALL of these criteria:

  • Be paid on a salary basis (not hourly)
  • Earn at least $684 per week ($35,568 annually)
  • Perform primarily executive, administrative, professional, computer, or outside sales duties as defined by regulations

Important Note

Job titles alone don't determine exempt status. The actual job duties and salary must meet specific legal criteria. Many employees are incorrectly classified as exempt.

Workplace Discrimination

Federal laws prohibit employment discrimination based on protected characteristics:

  • Race, color, and national origin
  • Sex (including pregnancy, sexual orientation, and gender identity)
  • Religion
  • Age (40 and older)
  • Disability
  • Genetic information

Many states and cities extend protections to additional characteristics such as marital status, political affiliation, or criminal history.

Types of Prohibited Discrimination

Employment discrimination laws cover:

  • Hiring and firing
  • Compensation and benefits
  • Job assignments and promotions
  • Training and development opportunities
  • Any other term or condition of employment

Harassment

Workplace harassment is a form of discrimination that includes:

  • Quid pro quo harassment: When job benefits are conditioned on submitting to unwelcome conduct
  • Hostile work environment: When unwelcome conduct is severe or pervasive enough to create an intimidating or offensive work environment

Reporting Discrimination

If you believe you've experienced workplace discrimination:

  1. Document incidents with dates, times, locations, and witnesses
  2. Follow your company's complaint procedures
  3. File a charge with the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) within 180 days (or 300 days in states with their own anti-discrimination laws)
  4. Consider consulting with an employment attorney

Family and Medical Leave

The Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA) provides eligible employees with unpaid, job-protected leave for specific family and medical reasons.
