Small Claims Court Guide

Navigate the small claims court process with confidence using our step-by-step guide to filing claims, preparing for court, and collecting judgments.

What is Small Claims Court?

Small claims court is a simplified court process designed to resolve civil disputes involving relatively small amounts of money. These courts have simplified rules, lower filing fees, and typically don't require attorneys, making them accessible to ordinary citizens seeking to resolve disputes.

Important Notes About Small Claims Court:

• Dollar limits vary by state (typically $3,000-$10,000)

• Cases are usually heard within 1-2 months of filing

• Most people represent themselves without attorneys

• Decisions are legally binding but appeal options may be limited

• Appropriate for disputes like unpaid debts, property damage, and minor contractual issues

When to Use Small Claims Court

Small claims court is typically appropriate for:

  • Unpaid personal loans or debts
  • Security deposit disputes with landlords
  • Property damage from accidents or negligence
  • Breach of contract for goods or services
  • Minor business disputes
  • Consumer complaints against businesses

Step-by-Step Guide to Filing a Small Claims Case

1
Attempt to Resolve the Dispute

Before filing a claim, make reasonable attempts to resolve the issue directly with the other party. Send a demand letter clearly stating what you want and why, providing a reasonable timeframe for response. Save all communications for potential evidence.

2
Determine the Correct Court Location

File your claim in the appropriate court jurisdiction. This is typically in the county where:

  • The defendant lives or does business
  • The incident occurred
  • The contract was signed or was to be performed
3
Complete and File Court Forms

Visit your local small claims court clerk's office or website to obtain the necessary forms. You'll typically need to provide:

  • Your complete contact information
  • The defendant's name and address
  • The amount you're claiming
  • A brief statement explaining your claim

Pay the filing fee, which typically ranges from $30-$100 depending on your location and claim amount.

4
Serve the Defendant

The defendant must be legally notified of your claim. Methods of service typically include:

  • Certified mail with return receipt
  • Personal service by sheriff or process server
  • Substituted service to someone at their residence or business

Service requirements vary by state, so check your local court rules for specific requirements.

5
Prepare for Your Court Date

Organize all evidence to support your claim, including:

  • Contracts, invoices, and receipts
  • Photographs of damaged property
  • Written communications (letters, emails, text messages)
  • Witness statements or arrangements for witnesses to appear

Prepare a clear, concise statement explaining your case. Practice explaining your situation in under 5 minutes.

6
Attend Your Court Hearing

On your court date:

  • Arrive early and dress professionally
  • Bring all evidence and multiple copies for the judge and defendant
  • Be polite and respectful to everyone, including the defendant
  • Listen carefully to the judge's questions
  • Speak clearly and stick to relevant facts
  • Never interrupt the judge or the other party

Collecting Your Judgment

Winning your case doesn't automatically mean you'll receive payment. If the defendant doesn't voluntarily pay, you may need to take additional steps:

Collection Options

  • Bank Levy: Seize funds from the defendant's bank account
  • Wage Garnishment: Have money deducted from the defendant's paycheck
  • Property Lien: Place a legal claim against the defendant's property
  • Till Tap: Seize money from a business's cash register
  • Judgment Debtor Examination: Force the defendant to appear in court and answer questions about their assets

Important Collection Tips:

• You'll need to complete additional paperwork for most collection methods

• Collection procedures vary significantly by state

• Some types of income (like Social Security) and property are exempt from collection

• You may need to pay additional fees for collection attempts

• Judgments typically expire after several years but can often be renewed

If the judgment amount is substantial or collection is proving difficult, consider consulting with an attorney who specializes in judgment collection.

If You're Being Sued in Small Claims Court

If you've been served with small claims court papers:

  • Read all paperwork carefully and note any response deadlines
  • Consider whether you have a valid defense or counterclaim
  • Gather evidence to support your position
  • Decide whether to attempt settlement before the hearing
  • Attend the hearing even if you believe you don't owe the money

Failing to appear at your hearing will likely result in a default judgment against you, which can be more difficult and expensive to resolve later.

Need Additional Help?

Small claims procedures vary by state and can change over time. For the most current information specific to your location:

  • Visit your local small claims court website
  • Contact your court clerk's office directly
  • Consider a consultation with a legal aid organization
  • Check if your court offers free small claims advisors

Remember that this guide provides general information only and is not a substitute for personalized legal advice.
