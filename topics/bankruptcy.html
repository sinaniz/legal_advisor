Understanding Bankruptcy

Learn about bankruptcy options, processes, and how they might affect your financial future.

What is Bankruptcy?

Bankruptcy is a legal process designed to help individuals and businesses eliminate or repay their debts under the protection of the federal bankruptcy court. It provides a fresh financial start for those unable to pay their debts, while also offering creditors a way to receive at least partial repayment.

Filing for bankruptcy is a significant financial decision that can have long-lasting effects on your credit score and financial future. While it can provide relief from overwhelming debt, it's important to understand the different types of bankruptcy, their requirements, and their consequences before proceeding.

Chapter 7 Bankruptcy

Liquidation Bankruptcy

Chapter 7 bankruptcy is often called "liquidation bankruptcy" because it involves selling certain assets to pay off creditors. However, many people who file Chapter 7 can keep most or all of their property through exemptions.

Key Features:

  • Typically completed in 3-6 months
  • Eliminates most unsecured debts (credit cards, medical bills)
  • Subject to means test qualification
  • Some assets may be sold to repay creditors
  • Remains on credit report for 10 years

Who It's For:

Individuals with limited income who have primarily unsecured debts and few assets that would not be exempt from liquidation.

Chapter 13 Bankruptcy

Reorganization Bankruptcy

Chapter 13 bankruptcy is often called "reorganization bankruptcy" because it involves creating a repayment plan to pay back all or part of your debts over a period of 3 to 5 years while keeping your assets.

Key Features:

  • Repayment plan lasting 3-5 years
  • Allows you to keep your property
  • Can stop foreclosure and give time to catch up on mortgage payments
  • Requires regular income to make plan payments
  • Remains on credit report for 7 years

Who It's For:

Individuals with regular income who want to keep their property while paying off debts over time, particularly those with valuable assets or who are behind on mortgage or car payments.

The Bankruptcy Process

  • Credit Counseling

    Before filing for bankruptcy, you must complete credit counseling with an approved agency within 180 days before filing. This counseling is designed to help you determine if bankruptcy is truly your best option.

  • Filing the Petition

    The bankruptcy process officially begins when you file a petition with the bankruptcy court. This includes detailed information about your finances, including assets, liabilities, income, expenses, and a list of all creditors.

  • Automatic Stay

    Once you file for bankruptcy, an "automatic stay" goes into effect. This prevents most creditors from continuing collection activities, including lawsuits, wage garnishments, and even phone calls.

  • Meeting of Creditors

    About a month after filing, you'll attend a meeting of creditors (also called a 341 meeting). The bankruptcy trustee will ask you questions about your bankruptcy forms and financial situation. Creditors may attend to ask questions but rarely do.

  • Dealing with Your Property

    In Chapter 7, the trustee may sell nonexempt property to pay creditors. In Chapter 13, you keep your property but pay its value through your repayment plan.

  • Financial Management Course

    Before receiving a discharge, you must complete a financial management course from an approved provider. This course aims to help you manage your finances better in the future.

  • Discharge

    If everything goes as planned, the court will grant a discharge of your debts. In Chapter 7, this typically happens 3-6 months after filing. In Chapter 13, it happens after you complete your repayment plan, usually 3-5 years.

Frequently Asked Questions

Will I lose all my property if I file for bankruptcy?
No, not necessarily. In Chapter 7 bankruptcy, you can keep "exempt" property, which varies by state but often includes basic necessities, some equity in your home and car, retirement accounts, and tools needed for your job. In Chapter 13 bankruptcy, you typically keep all your property but must pay the value of nonexempt items through your repayment plan.
How long will bankruptcy stay on my credit report?
Chapter 7 bankruptcy remains on your credit report for 10 years from the filing date, while Chapter 13 bankruptcy stays on your report for 7 years. However, the impact on your credit score diminishes over time, especially if you practice good credit habits after bankruptcy.
Which debts can't be discharged in bankruptcy?
Certain debts cannot be discharged in bankruptcy, including most student loans, most tax debts, child support and alimony, debts for personal injury caused by driving while intoxicated, and debts resulting from fraud or other wrongful actions.
Can I file for bankruptcy without a lawyer?
While it is possible to file for bankruptcy without a lawyer (known as filing "pro se"), it's not recommended, especially for Chapter 13 cases. Bankruptcy law is complex, and mistakes can lead to your case being dismissed or debts not being discharged. Most experts strongly recommend working with an experienced bankruptcy attorney.
Will my spouse be affected if I file for bankruptcy?
If you file for bankruptcy individually, your spouse's credit should not be directly affected. However, if you have joint debts, creditors can still pursue your spouse for those debts. In community property states, some complications may arise regarding shared property and debts. Couples can also file jointly for bankruptcy if it makes sense for their situation.
