Overview of Criminal Law

Criminal law refers to the body of laws that define conduct prohibited by the government because it threatens and harms public safety and welfare. It establishes punishment for criminal acts. The primary objective of criminal law is to maintain social order and protect the community from harm while ensuring fairness in the legal process.

Misdemeanors

Less serious crimes typically punishable by less than one year in jail, fines, probation, or community service.

Felonies

More serious crimes punishable by imprisonment for more than one year, substantial fines, or even death in some jurisdictions.

Infractions

Minor violations such as traffic tickets that typically result in fines rather than jail time.

Wobblers

Crimes that can be charged as either a misdemeanor or a felony, depending on circumstances and prosecutorial discretion.

Criminal law is distinct from civil law, which concerns disputes between individuals. In criminal cases, the government prosecutes an individual for violating laws designed to protect public safety.

Your Rights During Criminal Proceedings

The U.S. Constitution and state constitutions provide several important rights to protect individuals accused of crimes. Understanding these rights is crucial for anyone involved in the criminal justice system.

Constitutional Protections

  • Fourth Amendment: Protection against unreasonable searches and seizures
  • Fifth Amendment: Right against self-incrimination and double jeopardy
  • Sixth Amendment: Right to a speedy trial, impartial jury, confrontation of witnesses, and legal counsel
  • Eighth Amendment: Protection against excessive bail, excessive fines, and cruel and unusual punishment

Miranda Rights

When in police custody and under interrogation, you have the right to:

  • Remain silent
  • Be informed that anything you say can be used against you in court
  • Have an attorney present during questioning
  • Have an attorney appointed if you cannot afford one

Important Reminder

Always remember that you have the right to remain silent and to request an attorney when interacting with law enforcement. Never waive these rights without first speaking to an attorney.

The Criminal Process

Understanding the criminal process helps you navigate the justice system more effectively. Here's a general overview of the stages in a criminal case:

Investigation

Law enforcement investigates potential crimes by gathering evidence, interviewing witnesses, and potentially identifying suspects.

Arrest

When police have probable cause to believe a person committed a crime, they may make an arrest, taking the suspect into custody.

Booking

After arrest, suspects are "booked" - photographed, fingerprinted, and personal information is recorded.

Initial Appearance/Arraignment

The accused appears before a judge, is informed of charges, and may enter a plea. Bail may be set at this stage.

Pre-Trial Motions

Attorneys may file motions to dismiss charges, suppress evidence, or resolve other legal issues before trial.

Trial

If the case proceeds to trial, both prosecution and defense present evidence and arguments before a judge or jury, who then determines guilt.

Sentencing

If found guilty, the defendant receives punishment, which may include incarceration, probation, fines, or community service.

Appeals

Defendants may appeal convictions or sentences if there were legal errors in the process.

Common Criminal Offenses

Criminal offenses vary widely in severity and consequences. Here are some common categories:

Property Crimes

  • Theft/Larceny: Taking someone else's property without permission
  • Burglary: Unlawfully entering a structure with intent to commit a crime
  • Robbery: Taking property from a person using force or threat
  • Arson: Deliberately setting fire to property
  • Vandalism: Damaging or destroying someone else's property

Violent Crimes

  • Assault: Threatening or attempting to inflict injury
  • Battery: Unlawful physical contact or harm to another person
  • Domestic Violence: Assault or battery involving household or family members
  • Homicide: The killing of one person by another

White Collar Crimes

  • Fraud: Deception for financial or personal gain
  • Embezzlement: Theft of assets by someone in a position of trust
  • Money Laundering: Concealing the origins of illegally obtained money
  • Tax Evasion: Illegal avoidance of tax payment

Drug Offenses

  • Possession: Having illegal substances for personal use
  • Distribution/Trafficking: Selling or transporting illegal substances
  • Manufacturing: Creating illegal substances

Legal Defenses in Criminal Cases

Various legal defenses may be available depending on the nature of the charges and circumstances:

Common Defenses

  • Alibi: Evidence that you were elsewhere when the crime occurred
  • Self-Defense: Justification for using force to protect yourself
  • Insanity: Lack of mental capacity to understand the wrongfulness of actions
  • Duress: Being forced to commit a crime under threat
  • Entrapment: Being induced by law enforcement to commit a crime
  • Statute of Limitations: Time limit for prosecuting has expired
  • Lack of Evidence: Insufficient proof to establish guilt beyond reasonable doubt
  • Constitutional Violations: Evidence obtained through illegal searches or interrogations

Working With Your Defense Attorney

Be completely honest with your attorney about all facts and circumstances. Attorney-client privilege protects your communications, and your lawyer needs all information to develop the best defense strategy.

Record Expungement and Sealing

Criminal records can impact employment, housing, and other opportunities. Expungement and record sealing may help mitigate these consequences.

Expungement

Expungement legally destroys or erases a criminal record, making it as if the offense never occurred. Eligibility varies by state and offense type.

Record Sealing

Record sealing limits public access to your criminal record but doesn't completely remove it. It may still be accessible to law enforcement and certain employers.

Eligibility Factors

  • Type and severity of offense
  • Time elapsed since conviction or completion of sentence
  • Subsequent criminal history
  • Completion of probation or other requirements
  • State-specific laws and procedures

Juvenile Criminal Cases

The juvenile justice system differs from the adult system in philosophy, procedure, and outcomes.

Key Differences

  • Focus on rehabilitation rather than punishment
  • Different terminology (e.g., "delinquent acts" instead of "crimes")
  • Greater confidentiality and protection of records
  • Different sentencing options focused on education and treatment
  • Parents or guardians are typically involved in proceedings

Juvenile Record Sealing

Many states provide processes for sealing or expunging juvenile records when the individual reaches adulthood, assuming certain criteria are met.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I talk to the police if I'm accused of a crime?

Generally, it's advisable to politely decline to answer questions until you have an attorney present. Anything you say can be used against you, even seemingly innocent statements.

What's the difference between a public defender and a private attorney?

Public defenders are appointed by the court for defendants who cannot afford an attorney. While they are licensed attorneys, they often have high caseloads. Private attorneys are hired directly by clients and may have more time to dedicate to each case.

Can I be arrested without a warrant?

Yes, in certain circumstances. Police can arrest without a warrant if they have probable cause to believe you committed a crime, especially for felonies or crimes committed in their presence.

What happens if I can't afford bail?

If you cannot afford bail, you may remain in custody until your trial. However, alternatives may be available, such as bail reduction, release on recognizance, or using a bail bondsman service.

Can I represent myself in a criminal case?

While you have the right to represent yourself (known as "pro se" representation), it's generally not advisable for criminal cases due to their complexity and the serious consequences involved.
